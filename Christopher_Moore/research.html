#research interests#


plant-animal interactions

most of my scholarship and research has been centered around plant-animal interactions.  i fully accept that it is very reductionistic to take a community and break it down to its pairwise components.  one of my ultimate goals is to find a framework to extrapolate and predict from the pairwise interactions to explain community-level phenomena at ecological and evolutionary scales.


facilitation and antagonism

we are still living in a Malthusian paradigm, whereby we think that antagonistic interactions (for example, competition) are drivers of various systems like ecological communities and economies.  i understand this to be misleading given the ephemerality of competitive interactions and stabilization of +,- interactions on an evolutionary timescale.  what has been grossly understudied and appreciated are the positive interactions (for example, cooperation, altruism).  most of my dissertation research is focused on mutualistic interactions exchanging a service (seed dispersal) for a reward (seeds as food).  i think that these positive interactions have a great deal of potential to help understand ecological and evolutionary phenomena.


dispersal and movement

dispersal is the way in which life interacts with space, and is an emergent property in the strictest sense.  i find the multifarious ways in which this has manifested itself fascinating.  ecological (demographic) and genetical dispersal can be vastly different, which is why I aim to study the two to investigate what has been called “Slatkin’s Paradox.”


randomness and directionality

these complimentary philosophies are ubiquitous in biological systems.  i like to ponder the direction and magnitude of forces (biological or otherwise) and how much they do or do not explain.  when forces do not explain the phenomenon and patterns still emerge, well, that just tickles me to the bone.  understanding what is random and deterministic is at the heart of the scientific approach and allows clarity in the noisy universe.


conservation

ultimately, I would like my work to have applications to help managers and decision makers make appropriate choices, whether for conservation or restoration efforts.


plant and animal behaviour

in studying seed dispersal, the animal behaviour is apparent and implicit.  i am really interested in plant behaviour that is a consequence of animal-mediated dispersal.  for instance, chipmunks cache seeds in clumps: how do the plants respond to this high density?  how do they respond to kin, conspecifics, or congeners?  there is a growing body of literature studying plant behaviour that I think is greatly understudied by basic science (agricultural studies have been investigating these questions for at least centuries).


ecological networks

this new quantitative framework is paradigm-shifting in community ecology.  although other fields have better, larger datasets than those of the ecological kind, we can look for universalities between fields that study complex systems and I think that the evolutionary component of ecology can be drawn from to inform other fields where change is theoretically under-treated.


thermodynamical biology

all life is ultimately governed by the same physical laws and rules.  viewing ecological and evolutionary processes through the physical lens can help explain some of the phenomena we observe in ecology and evolution.  i think this will be a key to 21st-century biology.
